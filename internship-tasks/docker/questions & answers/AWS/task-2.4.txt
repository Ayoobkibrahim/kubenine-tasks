1. What is an Availability Zone?
An Availability Zone is an isolated physical data center within an AWS Region, designed with its own power and 
networking so that a failure in one AZ does not bring down the others.

example: 
The Region: Think of an AWS "Region" like a major city (for example, Mumbai or London).
The Availability Zones (AZs): Inside that city, AWS builds several massive, highly secure buildings. 
These individual buildings are the Availability Zones.



2. Why is Single-AZ architecture risky?
Single-AZ architecture is risky because it creates a single point of failure; if that one specific data center experiences an outage, 
your entire application goes completely offline because there is no backup infrastructure.

example:
A Single-AZ architecture means you are putting all of your web servers, databases, and network routing into one single physical data center building

The Risk:
Hardware eventually fails. Power grids go down. Internet cables get accidentally cut by construction workers. If any kind of localized disaster hits that one specific building, 
your entire application goes 100% offline until AWS fixes the physical problem. You have no safety net.


3. How does Multi-AZ improve availability?
Multi-AZ improves availability by duplicating your infrastructure across isolated data centers; if one goes offline, 
traffic is automatically routed to the healthy backup, preventing any downtime for the end user.

How it works in AWS:
putting all your web servers in one data center (Availability Zone A), you split them up. You put half in AZ-A and half in AZ-B.

The Disaster: A massive storm takes out the power grid for AZ-A. Those servers go dead.
The Recovery: AWS automatically detects the failure. The traffic director (Load Balancer) stops sending users to the dead AZ-A and instantly routes 100% of the traffic to your healthy servers in AZ-B.


4. What would happen if one AZ goes down?
The servers running inside AZ-A would immediately become unreachable. However, the traffic manager (like an AWS Load Balancer) will automatically detect that AZ-A is dead. 
It will instantly stop sending users there and route 100% of the traffic to your healthy servers in AZ-B. Because of this automatic failover, the end-user likely won't even notice there was a disaster.



5. Does Multi-AZ increase cost? Why?
Yes, Multi-AZ increases costs because you are paying to duplicate infrastructure (like running extra NAT Gateways or standby databases) 
and you incur AWS data transfer fees for traffic moving between the physical data centers.


6. Why is high availability critical in production systems?
High availability is critical in production because hardware failures are inevitable; High Availability ensures your application automatically survives those failures without going offline, 
protecting the company from lost revenue and damaged customer trust.
