1. What is the difference between target tracking scaling and step scaling?
Target tracking scaling automatically adds or removes tasks to keep a specific metric, like CPU utilization, consistently at a defined target percentage. 
Step scaling gives more granular control by letting define explicit step adjustments, such as adding exactly one task if CPU hits 50%, and two tasks if it hits 70%.

2. Why are minimum and maximum task limits important for auto scaling?
Minimum limits ensure application always has enough baseline capacity to remain highly available and serve incoming traffic immediately. 
Maximum limits act as a critical financial safety net, preventing runaway AWS costs from an infinite scale-out event caused by a massive traffic spike or a rogue application bug.

3. What CloudWatch metrics are commonly used to scale ECS services?
The two most common CloudWatch metrics used for ECS auto scaling are CPUUtilization and MemoryUtilization. 
These calculate the average resource consumption across all running tasks in service to help the system determine if more or less capacity is needed.

4. What is a cooldown period and what problem does it solve?
A cooldown period is a mandatory pause applied after a scaling action during which no further auto-scaling events can occur. This prevents "oscillation" or "flapping," 
a problem where the system rapidly spins tasks up and down before the newly launched tasks have had enough time to stabilize and affect the CloudWatch metrics.

5. What happens when ECS launches a new task — how does traffic reach it?
When ECS launches a new task, it automatically registers that specific task's IP address with the Application Load Balancer's target group. 
Once the new task boots up and successfully passes the ALB's health checks, the load balancer automatically begins routing live user traffic to it.

6. If CPU stays at 15% and your threshold is 20%, will scaling happen? What if it briefly spikes to 25% and drops back?
If CPU stays at 15%, no scaling out will occur because the 20% threshold has not been breached. If it briefly spikes to 25% and drops back immediately, 
scaling still likely won't happen; CloudWatch typically requires the metric to stay above the threshold for a sustained, predefined period (like 3 minutes) to prevent scaling off of momentary anomalies.

7. Why might you want different cooldown periods for scale-out vs scale-in?
Generally configure a very short scale-out cooldown to react rapidly to sudden traffic spikes, ensuring users don't experience latency or downtime. 
configure a much longer scale-in cooldown to conservatively remove tasks, ensuring traffic has genuinely dropped off and preventing the system from prematurely killing resources it might immediately need again.

8. When you update a service's task definition, how does ECS replace the running tasks?
ECS performs a rolling update by safely launching the new tasks alongside the old ones. Once the new tasks are fully running and actively passing health checks, 
ECS automatically deregisters the old tasks from the load balancer, drains their remaining connections, and terminates them to ensure zero downtime.
